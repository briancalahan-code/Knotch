## 1. What Knotch Sells and Why People Buy

### The Product

Knotch is a **content intelligence platform**. It measures whether a company's content marketing is actually working — which blog posts, videos, landing pages, and paid media are driving real business outcomes (pipeline, revenue, brand lift) versus just generating vanity metrics (page views, likes, shares).

**Core products:**

- **Knotch One** — The main SaaS platform. Connects content activity to business outcomes across owned and paid channels. This is what most deals are built around.
- **ACE (Agentic Content Engine)** — AI-powered content generation, optimization, and automation. Newer product, expanding into existing accounts.
- **K1** — Core measurement and analytics engine. Often the entry point for new customers.
- **JCI (Journey Content Intelligence)** — Maps the buyer's content journey. Used by teams that care about the full-funnel path, not just individual content pieces.
- **Content Catalysts** — Pre-built content templates aligned to buyer personas. Professional services add-on.
- **KSC (Knotch Strategic Consulting)** — Implementation, custom integrations, measurement frameworks. Services revenue.
- **AIQ / EQ/IQ** — Intelligence modules for buyer insights and content scoring. Expansion products.

### Why Companies Buy

Every buyer conversation comes back to one of these problems:

1. **"We spend millions on content and can't prove it works."** The CMO or VP Marketing is under pressure from the CFO or board to justify content marketing spend. They need attribution — not just "this blog got 10K views" but "this content influenced $2M in pipeline."

2. **"We have data everywhere but no single view."** Marketing teams use 4-8 analytics tools (Google Analytics, HubSpot, Marketo, social platforms, SEO tools). None of them talk to each other. The team spends 15+ hours/week manually pulling and reconciling reports.

3. **"We're about to lose budget."** Finance is threatening to cut content spend by 20-25% because marketing can't prove ROI. Or a new CMO has a 90-day mandate to rationalize the tech stack and prove every tool earns its keep.

4. **"We need to get smarter about what content to create."** The team produces 30-50+ pieces per month but has no systematic way to know what's working, what to double down on, and what to stop doing.

### The Buying Pattern

Knotch sells to **enterprise and upper-mid-market brands** with significant content marketing budgets. These are not startups or SMBs — they're companies with dedicated marketing teams, multiple content channels, and enough spend that proving ROI is a board-level conversation.

- **Average deal size:** $130K–$167K depending on company tier
- **Sales cycle:** 2–5 months (faster for mid-market, slower for large enterprise)
- **Renewal rate:** Very high — once installed, companies renew consistently
- **Expansion:** Customers often buy additional products (ACE, AIQ) after the initial K1/Knotch One deployment

## 2. Company Targeting

### The Three Tiers

Knotch scores and tiers every target account using a four-pillar ICP model. You don't need to know the scoring math — you need to know what each tier looks like and how to prioritize.

> **Deep dive:** Enablement/06-Territory-and-ICP-Tiering.md has the full tier definitions, territory rules, and rep allocations.

**Tier I — Large Enterprise (291 accounts)**

- Revenue: $2B+, Employees: 10,000+
- Has a CMO or VP Marketing with a dedicated content and demand gen team (5+ people)
- Runs enterprise marketing tools (Marketo, Eloqua, Adobe Experience Manager, Salesforce Marketing Cloud)
- Content strategy spans multiple regions, brands, or business units
- Longest sales cycle (~90+ days) but highest win rate and largest deal size

**Tier II — Mid-Enterprise (433 accounts)**

- Revenue: $500M–$2B, Employees: 2,000–10,000
- VP of Marketing leading a growing demand gen function (3–5 people)
- Mid-market marketing stack (HubSpot, Oracle, Pardot)
- 2–3 major content programs (thought leadership, demand gen, brand awareness)
- **Best overall economics** — fastest sales cycle (~60–75 days), highest expansion rate, best long-term value relative to initial deal size

**Tier III — Upper Mid-Market**

- Revenue: $100M–$500M, Employees: 500–2,000
- Marketing led by Director or Manager with a small team (1–3 people)
- Basic or partially implemented marketing tools
- Content strategy is early-stage or opportunistic
- Lower win rate and lower expansion potential — but still worth pursuing when signals are strong

### Firmographic Sweet Spots

These patterns come from analyzing which types of companies historically convert at higher rates. **These are directional signals, not hard rules** — always verify with your AE before deprioritizing any account.

**Company size (net new):**

- **1,000–10,000 employees** is the sweet spot for new business. Companies in this range tend to be large enough to have real content budgets and dedicated marketing teams, but not so large that procurement processes kill deals.
- Companies **under 500 employees** rarely have the content marketing maturity or budget that Knotch requires.
- **Very large companies (50,000+ employees)** are harder to close as net-new — they tend to have incumbent vendors, longer procurement cycles, and more internal politics. However, they make excellent expansion targets once you're already inside.

**Revenue:**

- **$100M–$10B** is the productive range for new business.
- Below $100M, companies generally don't have enough content spend to justify the investment.
- Above $50B, net-new deals face significantly more procurement friction and incumbent competition. The exceptions exist, but they require executive sponsorship and longer timelines.

**Industry verticals (strongest fit):**

- **Financial Services** — Banks, fintechs, payment platforms, wealth management, insurance. Large content budgets, regulatory pressure to measure everything, sophisticated marketing teams.
- **Technology / SaaS** — Content-heavy marketing orgs, strong digital presence, data-driven culture.
- **Consumer Packaged Goods (CPG) & Retail** — Multi-brand companies with distributed content teams. Brand measurement is critical.
- **Healthcare** — Medtech, pharma, health systems. Growing content investment, increasing regulatory need for measurement.
- **Media & Publishing** — Natural fit for content intelligence, though budgets can be tighter.

### Technographic Signals

The technologies a company uses tell you a lot about their marketing maturity and likelihood of buying Knotch. When you're researching an account, look for these:

**Strong positive signals (company invests in content and measurement):**

- **Content management platforms:** Contentful, WordPress (enterprise installations), Drupal, Sitecore, Adobe Experience Manager
- **Customer data platforms:** Segment, mParticle, Tealium
- **Marketing automation:** Marketo, Pardot, Eloqua, HubSpot Marketing Hub (enterprise tier)
- **Personalization tools:** Optimizely, Dynamic Yield, Monetate, Mutiny
- **Analytics maturity:** Multiple analytics tools (Google Analytics + Mixpanel, Amplitude, or Hotjar) suggests they care about measurement
- **Performance marketing pixels:** TikTok Pixel, Snap Pixel, Criteo — signals active digital spend and measurement orientation
- **Email infrastructure:** DKIM authentication, SendGrid, enterprise email tools — signals operational maturity

**Moderate signals (worth noting, not definitive):**

- Tag management (Google Tag Manager, Tealium) — organized data collection
- DAM platforms (Cloudinary, Bynder) — content production at scale
- A/B testing tools — measurement culture

**Negative signals (proceed with caution):**

- **Legacy tech stacks:** ASP.NET, Adobe CQ (legacy CMS), very old jQuery versions — these companies tend to move slowly, have brittle tech environments, and struggle to adopt new platforms
- **HR/recruiting tool dominance:** When the most prominent technology signals from a company are Greenhouse, Lever, or JobVite rather than marketing tools, the org is likely not content-marketing-forward
- **No content management or analytics tools visible:** If you can't find evidence of a CMS, analytics, or marketing automation platform, the company probably doesn't have the content infrastructure to benefit from Knotch

> **How to check:** When researching a company in Apollo or LinkedIn, look at the "Technologies" field. You can also check BuiltWith.com for any company's public-facing tech stack.

### Win/Loss Patterns by Company Type

These patterns are based on historical deal analysis. Sample sizes vary by segment — treat these as **directional guidance, not absolute rules**. When in doubt, ask your AE.

> **Data vintage:** Patterns derived from deals closed through Q1 2026. Verify with your AE for any account that feels borderline.

**Where Knotch wins (net new):**

- Mid-market companies (1K–10K employees) with modern marketing stacks and dedicated content teams
- Companies actively investing in content — visible through job postings for content roles, active blogs, heavy social media presence, presence of CDPs and personalization tools
- Companies with a triggering event: new CMO, vendor renewal coming up, board pressure on marketing ROI, budget defense season
- Accounts where we can identify and engage multiple stakeholders (3+ contacts) early in the process

**Where Knotch struggles (net new):**

- Mega-enterprise accounts with complex procurement — deals stall in legal/procurement for months
- Companies with entrenched incumbent analytics vendors (especially when the buyer group is small and the incumbent has deep integrations)
- Organizations that are still in "build mode" on basic marketing infrastructure — they need a CMS and email platform before they need content intelligence
- Single-threaded deals — when only one person at the company is engaged, the deal is fragile

**Where Knotch excels (expansion):**

- Existing customers renew at an extremely high rate — the product is sticky once deployed
- Expansion into new products (ACE for content generation, AIQ for buyer intelligence) is a significant growth driver
- Multi-deal velocity is strong at companies where the initial deployment succeeded — one win leads to additional departments or brands adopting
- The best expansion candidates are companies with multiple brands, business units, or regional marketing teams that could each be a separate deployment

Knotch has defined 15 buyer personas — these are the types of people you'll be researching and prospecting into. Each persona cares about different things and responds to different messaging.

### The 7 Buying Committee Roles

Every enterprise deal involves multiple people playing different roles. Understanding who plays what role is critical for account research and call prep.

| Role                    | What They Do                                                                      | When They Matter                                             | Seniority                        |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------- |
| **Economic Buyer**      | Signs the contract, controls the budget                                           | Must be identified by Qualification stage                    | VP or C-level                    |
| **Champion**            | Internal advocate — sells Knotch when you're not in the room                      | Must exist by Qualification (deal can't advance without one) | Director or Senior Manager       |
| **Executive Sponsor**   | Senior leader providing organizational air cover                                  | Identified by Consensus stage                                | VP or C-level                    |

**The key insight for your work:** Most deals that fail are **single-threaded** — only one contact engaged. Your job in account research is to identify who ELSE should be in the conversation beyond the person we're already talking to.

### The 4 Outbound Role Clusters

When you're building prospect lists in Apollo, target these four roles in this priority order:

**Cluster 1: Economic Buyer (highest priority)**

- Titles to search: CMO, VP Marketing, SVP Marketing, Head of Marketing, Chief Communications Officer, Chief Marketing Officer, VP Brand Marketing
- Seniority filter: VP, C-Suite
- Why first: Nothing happens without budget authority

**Cluster 2: Champion (Content/Demand Gen leader)**

- Titles to search: VP Content, Director Content, Head of Content, VP Demand Generation, Director Demand Generation, Content Marketing Director, Head of Content Marketing
- Seniority filter: Director, VP
- Why second: Champions sell internally — they're the ones who actually push the deal forward

**Cluster 3: Digital Co-Champion**

- Titles to search: VP Digital Marketing, Director Digital Marketing, Head of Digital, VP Digital Experience, Head of Performance Marketing, Director Digital Strategy
- Seniority filter: Director, VP
- Why third: Content intelligence spans owned and paid channels — the digital leader is often a co-champion alongside the content leader

**Cluster 4: Technical Validator**

- Titles to search: VP Marketing Ops, Director Marketing Ops, Head of MarTech, Marketing Technology Director, VP Marketing Technology, Director Marketing Analytics
- Seniority filter: Director, VP
- Why fourth: Technical validators rarely initiate a deal, but they can kill one. Getting them involved early (not late) prevents surprises at Procurement.

> **Deep dive:** Enablement/08 has the full outbound prospecting playbook with Apollo search instructions and sequence templates.

### Seniority Rules

- **VP and above** are your primary targets for outbound prospecting. They have budget authority or organizational influence.
- **Directors** are critical for Champion and Technical Validator roles. They're the operational decision-makers.
- **Managers** sometimes appear as Champions in mid-market companies, but rarely have budget authority in enterprise.
- **ICs and below** are not outbound targets — they may be End Users on a deal but won't initiate or approve a purchase.

The Knotch contact database is approximately: 9% Executive, 17% VP, 40% Director, and the rest Manager/IC. When building outbound lists, filter to Director+ unless specifically looking for End Users on an active deal.

### Personas in Winning vs. Losing Patterns

These are directional signals from historical deals:

- **Deals that win** tend to have **Marketing Leadership + Content/Demand Gen + one technical or digital stakeholder** involved. The combination of budget authority + internal champion + someone who validates the implementation is the winning formula.
- **Deals that lose** are often single-persona: only Marketing Leadership (no champion to push internally) or only Content/Demand Gen (no budget authority to close).
- **Finance persona involvement** is neither positive nor negative on its own — it's a signal that the deal is in later stages and budget scrutiny is happening (which is normal).
- **Legal/Procurement** involvement at early stages (before Proposal) is sometimes a negative signal — it can mean the company's procurement process is complex enough to slow things significantly.

## 4. What Resonates

### Pain Points by Persona

When you're prepping an AE for a call or writing account research notes, frame the opportunity around the pain the buyer persona cares about:

**Marketing Leadership (CMO / VP Marketing):**

- "We spend $X million on content and I can't prove it works to the board"
- "The CFO is threatening to cut my content budget 20-25%"
- "I have a new mandate to rationalize the tech stack and justify every tool"
- "We need board-ready dashboards, not spreadsheets"

**Content & Demand Gen:**

- "We produce 30+ pieces/month but don't know which ones drive pipeline"
- "My team spends 15+ hours/week pulling data from 4 different tools"
- "We have no way to compare owned content performance against paid media"
- "I can't tell my CMO which content to invest more in vs. cut"

**Digital Marketing:**

- "Our SEO and paid media teams have completely separate measurement"
- "We can't tie content engagement to actual conversions"
- "We're spending on TikTok, YouTube, and organic — but measuring them in three different systems"

**IT / Analytics / MarTech:**

- "Any new vendor needs to integrate with GA4, HubSpot, and our CDP"
- "We've been burned by platforms that promised easy setup and took 6 months"
- "Data governance and privacy compliance are non-negotiable"
- "I need to see the API docs and data model before we move forward"

**Finance:**

- "What's the total cost of ownership over 3 years?"
- "What's the payback period?"
- "How does this compare to what we're already paying for analytics tools?"

### Product Angles by Persona

| Persona              | Lead With                                              | Don't Lead With                        |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| Marketing Leadership | Board-ready ROI dashboards, competitive positioning    | Technical architecture, API specs      |
| Content & Demand Gen | Content performance measurement, audience intelligence | Pricing, procurement process           |
| Digital Marketing    | SEO/AEO impact, cross-channel attribution              | Brand measurement, consulting services |
| IT / MarTech         | Integration architecture, data governance, SOC 2       | Revenue metrics, board reporting       |
| Finance              | TCO, payback period, competitive ROI comparison        | Product features, content strategy     |
| Executive Leadership | Revenue acceleration, competitive intelligence         | Implementation details, team training  |

### Critical Events That Trigger Buying

When you're researching an account, these events signal a company is more likely to buy NOW:

**Highest urgency:**

- **New CMO or VP Marketing** (60-90 day window) — new leaders review and rationalize the tech stack; they want to prove impact fast
- **Budget defense season** (typically Q3-Q4 for calendar-year companies) — marketing leaders need ROI proof before the next budget cycle
- **Current analytics vendor contract renewal** — especially if the incumbent is raising prices significantly (a 30-40% price increase is a common trigger)
- **Board asked for content ROI metrics** — creates an 8-12 week deadline

**High urgency:**

- **Company announced content strategy investment** — new content hires, blog relaunch, new content verticals
- **Agency RFP completed** — the winning agency often requires unified measurement
- **Competitor adopted Knotch** — creates internal pressure to evaluate
- **Digital transformation initiative** — company investing in martech modernization

**Moderate urgency:**

- **Job postings for content or demand gen roles** — signals growing investment
- **New VP of Digital or Head of Performance Marketing hired** — measurement mandate often follows
- **Industry regulation change** — especially in financial services and healthcare, where compliance requires documented measurement

### Competitive Context

Knotch competes against:

- **Homegrown solutions** (most common) — companies cobbling together Google Analytics + spreadsheets + manual reporting. This is the biggest competitor — not another vendor, but the status quo.
- **Point solutions** — Contentsquare (digital experience analytics), Google Analytics 4 (free but limited attribution), various SEO tools (Conductor, Semrush, Ahrefs)
- **In-house data teams** — companies that believe they can build content attribution internally

**When the intern hears these in research:** Flag it in the call brief. An account that's evaluated and rejected a competitor is actually a strong lead — they've already identified the problem and are actively looking for solutions.

## 5. What Doesn't Work

These are patterns where Knotch historically struggles. **Use these as deprioritization signals, not hard disqualifiers.** If an account shows one of these patterns but has strong positive signals (active buying trigger, engaged champion), bring it to your AE — they may still want to pursue it.

> **Data vintage:** Patterns based on deals through Q1 2026. These should be re-validated quarterly as new deals close.

### Company-Level Red Flags

**Deprioritize (but don't auto-disqualify):**

- Companies under 500 employees or under $100M revenue — typically lack the content marketing maturity and budget. Exceptions exist in content-native industries (media, publishing, B2B SaaS).
- Very large conglomerates ($50B+ revenue) for **net-new** outreach — procurement processes are extremely long and incumbent vendors have deep relationships. However, these are excellent targets for expansion if we're already inside a division.
- Companies whose primary business is unrelated to content marketing (heavy manufacturing, mining, utilities with no consumer brand) — they don't have the use case.

**Strong red flags (bring to AE before investing time):**

- Legacy technology environments (ASP.NET, Adobe CQ/legacy CMS, outdated infrastructure) — these companies move slowly and often can't integrate modern SaaS platforms without significant IT involvement
- HR/recruiting tool-dominant tech profiles (Greenhouse, Lever, JobVite as the most visible tech, with minimal marketing tools) — signals the company is not content-marketing-forward
- No visible CMS, analytics, or marketing automation — if you can't find evidence the company does digital content marketing, they're probably not ready for content intelligence
- Company has recently gone through major layoffs in marketing — budget contraction makes a new vendor purchase unlikely

### Selling Approach Red Flags

- **Single-threaded deals** — When only one person at the company is engaged, deals are fragile. If you're building a call brief and can only find one contact, flag it and research additional stakeholders.
- **Leading with features instead of pain** — Knotch sells outcomes (ROI proof, budget defense, competitive positioning), not features (dashboards, integrations, APIs). When writing research notes, frame everything in terms of the buyer's problem, not Knotch's capabilities.
- **Skipping the Technical Validator** — Deals that avoid the IT/MarTech persona early often hit a wall at Procurement when technical concerns surface. When researching an account, always look for the Marketing Ops or MarTech lead.

### Industry Segments to Deprioritize

- **Early-stage startups** — Pre-revenue or pre-Series B companies rarely have the content operation or budget
- **Pure-play e-commerce without content marketing** — Companies whose digital presence is entirely product listings and transactions (no editorial content, no thought leadership)
- **Government and education** — Long procurement cycles, low budget, and different measurement frameworks

## 6. Your Workflow

### Tools You'll Need

Before you start, make sure you have access to:

| Tool                           | What You Use It For                                               | Who to Ask                     |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Apollo**                     | Building prospect lists, finding contacts, researching accounts   | Your manager                   |
| **HubSpot** (read access)      | Looking up existing contacts and companies, checking account tier | Your manager                   |
| **LinkedIn / Sales Navigator** | Account research, org chart mapping, news monitoring              | Your manager                   |
| **Slack**                      | Delivering briefs and lists to AEs                                | Get added to relevant channels |

### Checklist: Building a Prospect List

Use this when an AE asks you to find contacts at a set of target accounts, or when you're building outbound lists proactively.

1. **Get the target list.** Which accounts? What tier? What role clusters are we targeting?
2. **Check HubSpot first.** Do we already have contacts at this account? What's the account tier? Are there existing deals? Don't create duplicates.
3. **Search Apollo.** Use the title strings from Section 3's four outbound clusters. Filter: Director+ seniority, relevant persona titles, at the target company.
4. **Aim for 3-5 contacts per account.** Ideal mix: 1 Economic Buyer + 1 Champion + 1 Digital Co-Champion or Technical Validator. Don't settle for just one contact.
5. **Capture key fields.** For each contact: name, title, email, phone (if available), LinkedIn URL, persona classification, buying role.
6. **Check for red flags.** Does the company match any patterns from Section 5? If yes, note it but include the account — let the AE make the call.
7. **Submit for AE review.** Your first 2-3 lists should go through your AE or manager for a quality check before being used. This catches systematic errors early.

### Checklist: Account Research / Call Brief

Use this when an AE has a meeting coming up and needs background on the account and the people they're meeting with.

**Company research (10-15 minutes):**

1. **Account basics:** Company name, industry, employee count, annual revenue, HQ location
2. **ICP tier:** Check HubSpot for `account_tier` (I, II, or III)
3. **Marketing team:** Who runs marketing? Find the CMO/VP Marketing on LinkedIn. How big is the team? (Check job postings for marketing roles — active hiring = growing investment)
4. **Content presence:** Does the company have a blog? Content hub? Resource library? Active social media? This tells you how seriously they take content marketing.
5. **Tech stack:** Check Apollo or BuiltWith for marketing technologies. Flag positive signals (CDPs, personalization, marketing automation) and negative signals (legacy tech, no analytics tools).
6. **Recent news:** Any leadership changes? Funding rounds? Product launches? Strategic initiatives? Layoffs? Check LinkedIn, Google News, and the company's press page.
7. **Buying triggers:** Match news/signals against the critical events in Section 4. Is there a reason they'd buy NOW?

**People research (5-10 minutes per person):**

1. **Title and role:** What's their actual title? Map to one of the 15 personas (Section 3).
2. **Buying committee role:** Are they likely the Economic Buyer, Champion, Technical Validator, or something else?
3. **LinkedIn activity:** What do they post about? What do they engage with? This reveals their priorities and gives the AE conversation hooks.
4. **Tenure:** How long in this role? New hires (under 6 months) are more likely to be evaluating vendors.
5. **Previous companies:** Did they come from a company that uses Knotch or a competitor? This is gold for the AE.

**Call brief format (deliver to AE in Slack):**

```
## [Company Name] — Call Brief
**Meeting:** [Date/time, who's attending]
**Account Tier:** [I / II / III]
**Industry:** [vertical]
**Size:** [employees] employees, ~$[X] revenue

### Company Context
[2-3 sentences: what the company does, their content marketing presence, any recent news/triggers]

### People in the Meeting
- **[Name]** — [Title] | Persona: [X] | Likely role: [Economic Buyer/Champion/etc.]
  - [1-2 bullets: LinkedIn activity, tenure, relevant background]

### Signals & Triggers
- [Buying trigger if found]
- [Tech stack signal if relevant]
- [Competitive context if known]

### Questions / Risks
- [Anything the AE should ask about or be aware of]
```

### Handoff Protocol

- **Prospect lists:** Deliver in Slack with a summary line: "[X] contacts across [Y] accounts, [role clusters targeted], ready for review."
- **Call briefs:** Deliver in Slack DM to the AE at least 24 hours before the meeting.
- **Red flags or questions:** If you find something concerning during research (company in financial trouble, contact left the company, strong competitor presence), flag it immediately — don't wait for the brief.

### When to Escalate to Your AE

- An account looks like it should be disqualified based on Section 5, but has a strong positive signal (new CMO, recent content investment, competitor loss)
- You can't find any marketing leadership contacts at a target account
- An account appears to be an existing customer or has an active deal (check HubSpot first)
- A contact you're researching appears to have changed companies or roles recently
- You're not sure which tier an account belongs to or whether it fits the ICP
